1. Must Read / Watch / Listen This Week — Briefing window: May 2–6, 2026

  • Article The Best Leaders Embrace the Role of Supporting Character — Jamil Zaki, HBR. Zaki, Stanford psychologist and director of the Social Neuroscience Lab, builds a direct, evidence-backed counter to the founder-mode thesis. The supporting-character leader is not passive: they practice intellectual humility as a discipline, ask questions that genuinely open rather than guide, and actively connect each person's work to that person's own values rather than the organization's values. Zaki's data shows this approach produces better team outcomes than the hero model across multiple industries and team sizes. The implication for a hotel-membership director is precise: the best member enrollments and renewals happen when the member discovers their own reason to engage. The leader who stays in the supporting role during that conversation gets a better outcome than the one who performs conviction. (HBR, May 5, 2026)
  • Article How to Build a Superteam That Keeps Getting Better — Ron Friedman, HBR. Friedman isolates six specific, observable leader behaviors that separate teams with sustained high performance from those that peak and regress. None require exceptional talent; all require sustained discipline. The six: experiment during good conditions rather than only under pressure; surface problems before they compound; give feedback in learning terms rather than corrective terms; maintain genuine proximity to the actual work; invest in development before the return is visible; and make the helpers behind each team win as visible as the people who achieved the win. Read alongside Zaki's piece, this article provides the operational framework for what the supporting-character model looks like in practice. (HBR, May/June 2026)

2. By Thinker

Adam Grant

Grant's most useful current output is the combination of The Curiosity Shop and its adjacent media appearances. The April 28 Today show segment on the pre-mortem method is worth treating as a standalone tool. Grant's instruction is specific: pick a future date (say, six months from now), tell the team the plan has failed, and ask each person to write one specific reason why before anyone speaks. The written step is not administrative; it prevents social conformity from shaping responses before the room has heard what each person actually thinks. The exercise transforms dissent from a social risk into a structured expectation. Grant also notes that teams that run pre-mortems consistently are more likely to surface operational problems early, because the practice establishes that naming what might go wrong is a valued contribution rather than a threat to group morale. Full Curiosity Shop archive at adamgrant.net/podcasts/the-curiosity-shop/ and @thecuriosityshop on YouTube. (adamgrant.net, May 2026; NBC Today, April 28, 2026)

Brené Brown

Brown's April 28 Today segment makes a structural argument about people-pleasing that is worth unpacking carefully. Her position: people-pleasing in a leader is not personality-level over-agreeableness. It is a learned organizational behavior in which the leader systematically prioritizes their own avoidance of interpersonal discomfort over the team's access to honest information. The downstream consequence is specific: teams led by people-pleasers develop a learned silence around difficulty, because the implicit signal from the leader is that discomfort is not welcome. Problems then compound invisibly, because the social cost of raising them grows with every unremarked occurrence. Brown's structural corrective is not to encourage bluntness but to build explicit structures, team agreements, meeting formats, feedback norms, that make honesty the path of least resistance rather than the path of most friction. Her Dare to Lead podcast archive and workbook (brenebrown.com) remain the most complete operational guide to applying this. (brenebrown.com / Dare to Lead, May 2026; NBC Today, April 28, 2026)

Kim Scott

The Radical Candor podcast has found strong recent form by pairing Scott's framework with guests whose work extends it into adjacent territory. The episode with Robert Glazer (Compass Within, 2026) explored what happens when leaders substitute core values for situational rules: Glazer argues that values-based leadership is more resilient under pressure because values travel to contexts that rules cannot anticipate. The episode with Dr. Tomas Chamorro-Premuzic covered the authenticity trap: the conflation of authenticity as a goal with authenticity as an excuse for failing to develop. Scott's Radical Candor model (care personally, challenge directly) threads the same needle at the interpersonal level: genuine care motivates the challenge, and the challenge is what makes the care actionable. All episodes with show notes at radicalcandor.com/podcast; the YouTube channel (@RadicalCandor) has companion video for key episodes. (Radical Candor podcast, radicalcandor.com, April/May 2026)

Liz Wiseman

The updated Multipliers (HarperBusiness, May 5, 2026) is the anchor book release of this window. The empirical foundation: Wiseman's research across 150 leaders found that Multiplier leaders consistently get 1.97x the effective output of Diminisher leaders from the same pool of people. The practical finding is even more striking: most Diminisher behaviors come not from disengaged or careless leaders but from highly engaged, high-performing ones whose individual-contributor instincts have become a ceiling as their scope of responsibility has grown. The new material updates the AI-era context: in teams where autonomous systems handle routine cognitive tasks, the multiplier effect of releasing team intelligence is larger, and the diminisher effect of suppressing it is more costly, than in traditional team structures. Wiseman maintains a practitioner toolkit at thewisemangroup.com with self-assessment materials drawn from the book. (Wiseman Group / HarperBusiness, May 5, 2026)

Patrick Lencioni

At The Table has been exploring the culture-versus-cult distinction in recent episodes: Lencioni's argument is that a strong organizational culture necessarily repels as much as it attracts, and that the fear of repelling is what causes most leadership teams to dilute their culture until it can no longer serve as a genuine behavioral guide. Episode 266 revisited the surprising origin of the Ideal Team Player framework (humble, hungry, smart) and why the simplicity of the model is also its durability: it operates at the values level rather than the skill level, which makes it more predictive of long-term performance and more stable across role changes. For a director building a membership sales team where sustained relational work demands character consistency over time, the Ideal Team Player lens screens for the qualities that are hardest to develop and easiest to identify early. Biweekly episodes at thetablegroup.com/at-the-table/. (thetablegroup.com / At The Table, May 2026)

4. Long Reads

HBR HBR May/June 2026 Leadership Issue — Read the Zaki and Friedman pieces as a pair. Jamil Zaki's "The Best Leaders Embrace the Role of Supporting Character" makes the intellectual-humility case with behavioral research. Ron Friedman's "How to Build a Superteam That Keeps Getting Better" provides the operational complement: six specific, measurable leader behaviors that compound team performance over time, none of which require exceptional talent and all of which require sustained discipline. The May/June issue also includes "The CEO of UnitedHealthcare on Fixing What Frustrates Customers Most" (Tim Noel), which is directly relevant to how Accor Plus members experience membership: Noel's finding is that customer frustration concentrates at friction points rather than at product gaps, and that identifying and eliminating those friction points is a more effective retention strategy than improving core benefits. Worth 20 minutes on a Sunday evening to read all three. (HBR, May 5, 2026; HBR, May/June 2026; HBR May/June issue)
McKinsey The State of Organizations 2026: Three Tectonic Forces Reshaping Organizations. Built on more than 10,000 senior executive interviews across 15 countries and 16 industries. The three forces: AI and technology acceleration, economic and geopolitical disruption, evolving employee expectations. The headline leadership finding: 75% of organizations fail to build high-performance cultures, and the gap correlates with whether leaders adopt a reflective, inside-out approach rather than a transactional, outside-in one. Human-centric leadership is associated with 56% improvement in retention, 56% improvement in trust, and 42% improvement in decision quality. Three decisions McKinsey identifies for now: commit to human-centric leadership as a performance strategy rather than a values statement; build AI adoption capability at the team level rather than the tool level; and redesign work structures to match evolving employee expectations. UNLEASH published a 10-minute synthesis if you want the short version before committing to the full 74-page report. (McKinsey, 2026; UNLEASH synthesis, May 2026)
MIT Sloan Managing Up: A Skill Set That Matters Now — Phillip G. Clampitt and Bob DeKoch, MIT Sloan Management Review. A well-timed piece for any director operating inside a large organization who wants to be genuinely useful to the decision-makers above them rather than merely responsive. Clampitt and DeKoch argue that managing up is not political maneuvering; it is a translational discipline with three learnable core competencies: calibrating what your leader needs to know versus what they want to know; translating team-level operational problems into organization-level strategic opportunities; and building the credibility required to push back on direction with authority rather than anxiety. Each competency is described with specific observable behaviors. Applicable immediately for anyone who has felt the gap between what they know at the ground level and what reaches decision-makers above. (MIT Sloan Management Review, April 13, 2026)
Stanford Stanford Launches the Stanford Leadership Institute at GSB — Stanford Report, May 2026. Backed by a foundational gift from Diane and Andreas Halvorsen (MBA '90) and led by Maria Frantz (MBA '94) with Professor Ken Shotts as inaugural Velez Reyes Director, the institute is designed as an interdisciplinary hub drawing from across Stanford's schools. Dean Sarah Soule's framing: leaders need to move "from reacting to change toward shaping it," and the institute will generate research, programs, and convenings to support that transition. The April 15 Stanford Leadership Forum featured former UK Prime Minister Rishi Sunak (MBA '06), Citadel founder Ken Griffin, and PG&E CEO Patti Poppe. Track the institute's research output at gsb.stanford.edu/leadership-institute/about/news; the first substantive publications are expected in H2 2026. (Stanford Report, May 2026; Stanford Leadership Institute Events)
Poets&Quants / Stanford GSB Stanford GSB Dean Sarah Soule on AI, Leadership, and Why Human Skills Matter More Than Ever — Poets&Quants, April 22, 2026. One of the clearest published cases for continued investment in leadership development during a period when many organizations are cutting it. Soule's argument: as AI absorbs routine cognitive work, the premium shifts to capabilities that are genuinely human and genuinely difficult to replicate. She names four: ethical judgment under genuine ambiguity (not rule-following), coalition-building across competing interests, tolerance for sustained ambiguity without premature resolution, and the ability to ask better questions rather than provide faster answers. Each is a capability that grows through deliberate practice and shrinks through disuse. Under 15 minutes to read; worth returning to any time someone in your organization argues that leadership development is a nice-to-have. (Poets&Quants, April 22, 2026)
