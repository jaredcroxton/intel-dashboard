3-Line Executive Summary

The supporting-character model of leadership just got HBR's imprimatur: Jamil Zaki's May 5 article argues, with research, that leaders who step out of the hero role - practicing intellectual humility and helping people connect their work to their own values - outperform those who stay in founder mode. For a director whose core product is human relationships (the member conversation, the renewal, the referral), this framing maps directly onto how the best operators in membership businesses talk to their people.

Liz Wiseman's revised Multipliers drops today and is mandatory reading for anyone building a team at pace: The new edition adds the concept of the accidental diminisher - the well-intentioned, high-performing leader who caps team intelligence by solving problems too quickly or having opinions too early. McKinsey's concurrent State of Organizations 2026 report shows 75% of organizations fail to build high-performance cultures; Wiseman's framework is the most practical correction available.

Two of the most followed voices in leadership thinking just launched a joint weekly show: Adam Grant and Brené Brown's Curiosity Shop podcast (Vox Media, weekly on Thursdays) is the format story of 2026 - quantitative and qualitative, disagreement modeled in public, useful tension every week. Stanford is simultaneously launching a formal Leadership Institute at GSB. The structural investment in leadership education is accelerating, and the quality of the content available to you this week reflects it.

1. Must Read / Watch / Listen This Week — Briefing window: May 1–5, 2026

  • Article The Best Leaders Embrace the Role of Supporting Character. Jamil Zaki's HBR piece lands today as a direct, research-grounded answer to two years of "founder mode" discourse. The argument: leaders who position themselves as supporting characters - listening more, asking better questions, practicing intellectual humility, and actively connecting each team member's work to their own values - consistently outperform those who stay in the spotlight. The implication for a hotel-membership director is sharp: the member conversation works the same way. The best enrollments happen when the member discovers their own reason to join, not when the director is performing. (HBR, May 5, 2026)
  • Book Multipliers: Revised and Updated — Liz Wiseman. The definitive framework for the gap between leaders who double their team's effective output and those who unknowingly halve it, published today in its updated edition. The new chapter on "accidental diminishment" is the most immediately useful addition: it names the well-intentioned behaviors (rescuing too quickly, being overly optimistic, having opinions before the team has thought) that suppress team intelligence in leaders who would be horrified to know they do it. If your team's output feels capped, this book identifies exactly which leadership behavior is the ceiling. (Wiseman Group / HarperBusiness, May 5, 2026)
  • Podcast How Great Companies Stay Great — Radical Candor with Kim Scott and Eric Ries. Scott interviews Eric Ries (Lean Startup) about his forthcoming book "Incorruptible: Why Good Companies Go Bad and How Great Companies Stay Great," released this month. Ries's central finding: companies that survive their own success do so by structurally protecting their founding purpose from the forces that push toward mediocrity - investor pressure, bureaucratic comfort, the gradual substitution of shareholder value for original mission. The 62-minute episode connects directly to Scott's Radical Candor framework: the interpersonal version (care personally, challenge directly) is the micro-scale equivalent of what Ries argues organizations need at the structural level. (Radical Candor, April 29, 2026)
  • Article How to Build a Superteam That Keeps Getting Better — Ron Friedman, HBR. Research across thousands of teams to find what separates sustained high-performers from one-season wonders. The differentiator is not talent acquisition but the culture leaders build around learning. Specifically: the best teams experiment even when things are going well (not only when they're under pressure), surface problems before they compound, stay proximate to the actual work, and invest in development before the ROI is visible. Every item is a specific, observable leadership behavior - not a trait or a sentiment. (HBR, May/June 2026)
  • Video The Curiosity Shop with Brené Brown and Adam Grant — Vox Media / YouTube. The most consequential new format in leadership content this year. Brown (qualitative researcher, shame and vulnerability) and Grant (organizational psychologist, evidence and rethinking) in direct, unscripted disagreement and curiosity, weekly, in video and audio. Their standing intellectual disagreements produce more useful friction than most leadership content achieves in a season. The show models what high-trust professional disagreement actually looks like in practice - useful direct observation for any leadership team trying to build psychological safety. New episodes every Thursday on YouTube (@thecuriosityshop) and all podcast platforms. (The Curiosity Shop / Vox Media, YouTube, May 2026)

Simon Sinek

Two pieces this week at simonsinek.com. (1) A story published today (May 5) on why we delay the conversations we know we need to have, with the observation that waiting is not neutral: every day of delay is a withdrawal from the relationship, not a pause. Directly applicable to the difficult conversations that define team performance: feedback, performance concerns, misaligned expectations. (2) His "One Leadership Habit That Will Transform Your 2026" piece recommends one specific ritual: every time someone on your team has a win, ask publicly, "Who helped you?" Then name and recognize those helpers. The practice redistributes credit, signals that interdependence is valued, and makes invisible support visible - three culture outcomes from a single question. (simonsinek.com, May 5, 2026; Simon Sinek, One Leadership Habit)

Liz Wiseman

The revised and updated Multipliers: How the Best Leaders Make Everyone Smarter publishes today (May 5, 2026) from HarperBusiness. The original framework distinguished between Multiplier leaders (those who amplify collective intelligence, often getting 2x output from the same people) and Diminishers (those who unintentionally reduce it). The most important addition in the new edition is the "accidental diminisher" concept: the high-performing, well-intentioned leader who suppresses team intelligence by being too quick to solve, too visibly optimistic to allow honest assessment, or too expert to leave room for others to think. This is the leadership failure mode most common at the director level and above - and the one least likely to be recognized by the person doing it. (Wiseman Group / HarperBusiness, May 5, 2026)

4. Long Reads

McKinsey The State of Organizations 2026: Three Tectonic Forces Reshaping Organizations. Based on more than 10,000 senior executive interviews across 15 countries and 16 industries. The three forces: AI and technology acceleration, economic and geopolitical disruption, evolving employee expectations and work models. The leadership finding that matters most: 75% of organizations fail to build high-performance cultures, and the gap correlates strongly with whether leaders adopt a reflective, inside-out approach. Human-centric leadership drives 56% improvement in employee retention, 56% in trust, and 42% in decision-making quality. Three specific organizational decisions identified for leaders to make now. Long executive read; also available as a podcast summary episode on Spotify. (McKinsey, 2026; HRZone summary, May 2026)
HBR HBR May/June 2026 Leadership Issue: Two essential reads this cycle. (1) "How to Build a Superteam That Keeps Getting Better" by Ron Friedman isolates six specific, executable leader behaviors that compound team performance over time. (2) "The Best Leaders Embrace the Role of Supporting Character" by Jamil Zaki challenges founder-mode assumptions with research on what actually produces better outcomes at the team level. Both are available at hbr.org. The May/June issue also includes "The CEO of UnitedHealthcare on Fixing What Frustrates Customers Most" — useful reading on customer-centricity in membership and subscription businesses. (HBR, May 2026; HBR May/June issue)
Stanford Stanford Launches the Stanford Leadership Institute at GSB. Stanford announced this month the creation of a formal leadership institute at the Graduate School of Business, designed to prepare leaders for the forces reshaping business and society by leveraging expertise from across the university. Dean Sarah Soule's framing: "Effective leadership requires an understanding of the rapid forces of change facing businesses and society" and leaders need to move "from reacting to change toward shaping it." The institute will produce research, programmes, and content. Worth watching for output in H2 2026 and into 2027. (Stanford Report, May 2026; Stanford GSB Insights, Leadership)
Poets&Quants / Stanford GSB Stanford GSB Dean Sarah Soule on AI, Leadership and Why Human Skills Matter More Than Ever. In a substantive April 22 interview, Dean Soule argues directly against the assumption that AI commoditizes leadership: the more AI takes over routine cognitive work, the more the premium shifts to specifically human capabilities — ethical judgment, coalition-building, ambiguity tolerance, and the ability to ask better questions. One of the clearest executive-level arguments for continued investment in leadership development at a time when many organizations are cutting it. (Poets&Quants, April 22, 2026)
UNLEASH McKinsey's State of Organizations 2026: Three Decisions to Make Now. UNLEASH's editorial synthesis of the McKinsey report, focused specifically on the HR and people leadership implications. More accessible than the full report, with a sharper focus on the talent, culture, and workforce shifts. The three decisions McKinsey identifies: (1) commit to human-centric leadership as a performance strategy, not just a values statement; (2) build AI adoption capability at the team rather than the tool level; (3) redesign how work is structured to match evolving employee expectations. Under 10 minutes to read; useful preparation before reviewing the full McKinsey report. (UNLEASH, May 2026)
